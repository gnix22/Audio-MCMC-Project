\section{Markov Chain Monte Carlo}
Before expressing the formulation of MCMC, a look into the works of Bayes and Ulam are needed in order to understand the evolution into the methods seen in Bayesian statistics in the modern era. That is, these works are the foundational stepping stones that provide a path into what is now the Monte Carlo Method. Generally speaking, Monte Carlo methods were introduced during World War II in the Los Alamos National Laboratory, where these methods would later be used to form the Metropolis algorithm by the early 1950s \cite{Robert_2011}. Now, jumping backwards some 210 years prior, Bayes' first developed what would later become a published theorem, used in all of Bayesian statistics today; it is stated mathematically as 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:1}
    P(A|B)=\frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)},
\end{equation}
where $A,B$ are events such that $P(B)\neq 0$. Here, $P(A|B),P(B|A)$ are conditional probabilities, where $A$ and $B$ depend on the occurence of the other. Now, without going any further into the extensions of Bayes' Theorem, it is noted that Equation (\ref{eq:1}) was used in order to compute the distribution of an unknown probability parameter $\theta$ of a binomial distribution. This is the main contrast from that of the frequentist's perspective; the parameters of a distribution are unknown, and the most likely distribution for those parameters that describe the data are to be found. Thus, Equation (\ref{eq:1}) is reexpressed as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:2}
    P(\theta|D)=\frac{P(D|\theta)P(\theta)}{P(D)}.
\end{equation}
Equation (\ref{eq:2}) assumes the parameters of a distribution depend on the data, $P(\theta|D)$ (called the posterior), and this probability is informed by the likelihood $P(D|\theta)$, given a prior assumption, $P(\theta)$, about the parameters before any new data ($P(D)$) is observed. In other words, MCMC allows for a sampling from any distribution that can not be directly sampled, in essence, sampling from a prior distribution over $\theta$.
\subsection{Metropolis-Hastings Algorithm}
Now, there are many ways of implementing MCMC; a particular implementation is that of the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm. Essentially, what happens is that given a proposal distribution, denoted $Q(\theta'|\theta)$, helps to draw samples from a posterior distribution $P(\theta|D)$ that is refractory. This works by using $Q$ to perform a random walk within the parameter space, where the transition into a new state is accepted or rejected based on how likely the sample is to be observed. This process is Markovian in nature, hence the ``Markov Chain'' in MCMC. The likelihood of each new observation is derived from a function $f$, thus $f$ must be proportional to $P(\theta|D)$. The random walk of $Q$ is formed by a sample drawn from typically from a symmetric distribution proposed by new parameter $\theta'$, given the current parameter $\theta$. That is, $Q(\theta'|\theta)$ typically follows some distribution of symmetry such as $Q(\theta'|\theta)=\mathcal{N}(\mu,\theta)$. To decide the acceptance of $\theta'$, the ratio 
\begin{eqnarray*}
    \frac{P(\theta'|D)}{P(\theta|D)}&=&\frac{P(D|\theta')P(\theta')}{P(D|\theta)P(\theta)} (\text{Bayes' Formula})\\
                                    &=&\frac{\prod_{i}^{n}f(d_i|\Theta=\theta')P(\theta')}{\prod_{i}^{n}f(d_i|\Theta=\theta)P(\theta)}
\end{eqnarray*}
which leads to the probability that 
\begin{equation}
    P(\text{accept})=
    \begin{cases}
        \frac{\prod_{i}^{n}f(d_i|\Theta=\theta')P(\theta')}{\prod_{i}^{n}f(d_i|\Theta=\theta)P(\theta)}, \prod_{i}^{n}f(d_i|\Theta=\theta)P(\theta) > \prod_{i}^{n}f(d_i|\Theta=\theta')P(\theta')\\
        1, \prod_{i}^{n}f(d_i|\Theta=\theta)P(\theta) \leq \prod_{i}^{n}f(d_i|\Theta=\theta')P(\theta').
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
What Equation (2) states is that if $\theta'$ is a strong proposal for the next state, it is certain that $\theta'$ is chosen. If it is strictly lesser to the current $\theta$, it may still be accepted, however, with decreasing probability. Algorithm \ref{alg:1} provides a psuedocode representation of how this may be implemented \cite{Book_MonteCarlo_2004_Robert_MonteCarloStatisticalMethods}.

\begin{algorithm}[H]\label{alg:1}
\caption{Metropolis-Hastings Algorithm}
Initialise a state $\theta_0$;
Set $t=0$;

\For{$t$ \KwTo $N$}{
    1. Sample candidate $\theta'$ from proposal distribution $Q(\theta'|\theta_{t})$\;
    
    2. Calculate the acceptance ratio: 
    \[
    \alpha = \min\left(1, \frac{P(\theta') Q(\theta_t|\theta')}{P(\theta_{t}) Q(\theta'|\theta_{t})}\right);
    \]\
    
    3. Generate $u \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$\;
    
    \If{$u \le \alpha$}{
        Accept the proposal: $\theta_{t+1} \leftarrow \theta'$\;
    }
    \Else{
        Reject the proposal: $\theta_{t+1} \leftarrow \theta_{t}$\;
    }
    Increment $t=t+1$;
}
\end{algorithm}
\section{Implementation of MCMC to Audio}
The data used in implementation in this study comes from a personal favorite musical artist, that is an artist by the name of Porter Robinson. A sample of piece \textit{Divinity} is taken for study. The sample is at a duration 0.065s at 11.25khz. Figure 1 provides a visual representation of the data used.

    \centering

    \caption{Dual plot of the audio waveform and distribution of amplitudes}
    \label{fig:1}

As audio generally tends to be classified as sparse data, where the sample representation are zero \cite{Plumbleyetal2010}, a distribution with the capacity to model such representations is needed. Other studies have found that a Laplace distribution can be used in order to achieve this \cite{Sun2025-va}. The Laplace PDF is defined by 
\begin{equation}
    f(x|\mu,b)=\frac{1}{2b}e^{-\frac{|x-\mu|}{b}},
\end{equation}
where $\mu \in(-\infty,\infty),b \in [0,\infty)$ are location and scale parmeters, respectively. Using this as the prior distribution of the MCMC, the likelihood function is defined as 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lik}
    L(\mu,b|x)=\prod_{i=1}^{n}\frac{1}{2b}e^{-\frac{|x_i-\mu|}{b}}=\left(\frac{1}{2b}\right)^{n}\exp \left(-\frac{1}{b}\sum_{i=1}^{n}|x_i-\mu|\right).
\end{equation}
Taking the logarithm of Equation \ref{eq:lik}, obtained is
\begin{equation}
    \ell(\mu,b|x)=-n\ln(2b)-\frac{1}{b}\sum_{i=1}^{n}|x_i-\mu|.
\end{equation}
This posterior is then used in conjunction with Algorithm \ref{alg:1}, in order to sample the distribution of parameters for the data. Further implementation for choice of prior and proposal distribution can be found on the Github Repository associated with this study.
